\begin{abstract}
Production LLM inference systems increasingly deploy a \emph{front-door router}---a small language model (SLM) that classifies incoming prompts by task type and complexity, then routes each request to an appropriately capable (and priced) downstream model. The router is itself a model-selection problem: cheaper SLMs offer low latency but may fail at nuanced task-family boundaries; stronger SLMs improve classification quality but violate latency budgets or introduce external API dependencies. We report two completed studies and one pre-registered design for the \plexor{} front-door stack.
\textbf{Study~1 (Pilot Benchmark):} A locked, hardware-controlled pairwise comparison of Phi-3.5-mini (3.8B) and Qwen2.5-1.5B on a fixed 60-case corpus with a pre-registered JSON-safe decoding contract. On RunPod Secure A40, Phi-3.5-mini achieves significantly higher exact-label accuracy ($0.517$ vs.\ $0.200$; exact McNemar $p = 0.0013$, $\Delta = +0.317$, 95\% CI $[0.15, 0.48]$), while Qwen2.5-1.5B is substantially faster (median latency $1{,}017$\,ms vs.\ $12{,}227$\,ms; Wilcoxon $p = 1.63 \times 10^{-11}$). Family-level analysis reveals a \emph{split policy}: Phi dominates code-complex and chain-of-thought families; Qwen dominates hybrid-agentic prompts.
\textbf{Study~2 (Generational Bridge):} A same-prompt evaluation of Qwen-2.5-3B-Instruct (3B, 4-bit quantized, vLLM 0.17.1) on a reconstructed version of the same 60-case corpus, executed on an Azure T4 GPU. Qwen-2.5-3B achieves 0.767 overall accuracy ($46/60$), a $+0.567$ absolute improvement over its 1.5B predecessor and a $+0.250$ improvement over Phi-3.5-mini, with perfect parse rate and $1{,}564$\,ms mean latency.
\textbf{Study~3 (Four-Arm RCT Design):} A pre-registered randomized controlled trial comparing three candidate SLM backends---Phi-4-mini-instruct (Azure AI Foundry serverless), Qwen-2.5-3B-Instruct (self-hosted vLLM), and DeepSeek-V3 (commercial API)---against a no-routing control, with $N \geq 250$ per arm, Bonferroni-corrected pairwise tests ($\alpha/6 = 0.0083$), O'Brien-Fleming interim analysis at $N = 125$, and a pre-registered decision matrix with Pareto frontier analysis. Design complete; execution pending. Study~3 presents the pre-registered protocol; a companion paper will report execution results.
Together, these studies provide an end-to-end empirical path from offline model comparison through generational validation to production-grade experimentation. All run IDs, corpus hashes, and reproducibility manifests are released.

\textbf{Keywords:} model routing, small language models, front-door classification, randomized controlled trial, MLflow, latency-quality tradeoff, Pareto frontier, reproducibility
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The cost structure of large language model (LLM) inference creates a natural incentive for \emph{front-door routing}: before an expensive frontier model processes a request, a cheaper model classifies the incoming prompt by task type and complexity, then routes the request to an appropriately capable---and appropriately priced---downstream model \citep{jiang2023routellm,shnitzer2023large,chen2023frugalgpt}. This architecture has become standard in production LLM deployments, where a single percentage point of routing accuracy can translate to thousands of dollars per day in inference cost savings \citep{lu2023routing}. The underlying economic logic is straightforward: if a \$0.001-per-query classifier can correctly identify that 40\% of incoming traffic requires only a \$0.01-per-query model rather than a \$0.10-per-query frontier model, the cost savings compound rapidly at scale. At 100,000 queries per day, even a modest routing accuracy improvement of 5 percentage points yields annualized savings exceeding \$150,000, dwarfing the classifier's own operational cost.

The front-door is itself a model-selection problem. Cheaper small language models (SLMs) in the 1--4B parameter range offer sub-second latency but can fail at nuanced task-taxonomy boundaries; stronger SLMs or even full-scale models improve classification quality but may violate latency budgets, introduce external API dependencies, or negate cost savings by consuming the very inference budget they are meant to protect \citep{chen2023frugalgpt}. This tradeoff is amplified when teams require both \emph{scientific validity}---paired significance tests, dataset immutability, reproducibility---and \emph{operational validity}---health checks, logging schema, alertability, arm isolation. The tension between these two modes of validity is rarely acknowledged in the routing literature, where most work evaluates routing quality in isolation from the operational infrastructure that must sustain it in production \citep{madaan2023automix}.

The challenge is further compounded by the rapid pace of SLM development. The transformer architecture \citep{vaswani2017attention} has been adapted into increasingly efficient small-scale variants, from the Phi family's textbook-quality training data approach \citep{gunasekar2023phi1,li2023phi15,javaheripi2023phi2,abdin2024phi3,abdin2024phi4} to the Qwen family's multilingual scaling strategy \citep{yang2024qwen2,yang2024qwen25} and the open-weight ecosystem of Llama \citep{touvron2023llama2,meta2024llama3}, Gemma \citep{gemma2024gemma2,gemma2024gemma}, Mistral \citep{jiang2023mistral,jiang2024mixtral}, TinyLlama \citep{zhang2024tinyllama}, StableLM \citep{zhu2024stablelm}, and the TinyStories line of research demonstrating surprisingly coherent generation from sub-billion-parameter models \citep{eldan2023tinystories}. Each new release reshuffles the Pareto frontier of quality versus cost, rendering previously optimal routing configurations suboptimal within months. This ``model churn'' problem means that any static evaluation of routing models is inherently time-limited---a finding that motivates our multi-study design, which progresses from a locked offline benchmark through a generational bridge experiment to a pre-registered RCT that can accommodate future model candidates.

Article~1 in this series \citep{johnson2026compress} demonstrated a fundamental dichotomy: code-generation tasks benefit from compression-first strategies (Cohen's $d = 1.84$ over 2,650 trials), while chain-of-thought reasoning benefits from routing to appropriately-capable models. That finding established \emph{routing} as a first-class cost-reduction mechanism parallel to compression. But it left open the question of \emph{which model performs the routing itself}---the subject of the present article. The distinction is important: while prior work on learned routing \citep{jiang2023routellm,ong2025routellm} trains a dedicated classifier on preference data, and cascade architectures \citep{madaan2023automix,yue2024cascades} invoke multiple models per query, our front-door approach requires a single SLM to make an interpretable, discrete classification decision using only the raw prompt text. This constraint---single model, single pass, discrete output---places unique demands on SLM capability that differ from the generative and reasoning tasks on which SLMs are typically benchmarked \citep{hendrycks2021mmlu,clark2018arc,zellers2019hellaswag,bigcode2023evaluation,austin2021mbpp}. The question of which SLM should serve as the front-door classifier also intersects with emerging work on test-time compute scaling \citep{snell2024testtime} and speculative decoding \citep{leviathan2023speculative,chen2023speculative}, suggesting that the traditional tradeoff between model size and inference speed may be more nuanced than a simple parameter-count comparison implies.

We contribute three complementary studies spanning the full path from offline evaluation through production deployment design: (1) a \textbf{locked offline benchmark} (Study~1, \S\ref{sec:methods1}--\S\ref{sec:results1}) comparing two SLMs under hardware-controlled conditions with paired statistical tests on a 60-case corpus; (2) a \textbf{generational bridge experiment} (Study~2, \S\ref{sec:methods2}--\S\ref{sec:results2}) evaluating whether a single generation jump in the Qwen family (1.5B $\to$ 3B) resolves the accuracy deficit observed in Study~1; and (3) a \textbf{pre-registered four-arm RCT design} (Study~3, \S\ref{sec:rct-design}) with Bonferroni correction, O'Brien-Fleming interim analysis, and a Pareto frontier decision matrix. Beyond the empirical findings, this paper makes three methodological contributions: a locked-contract evaluation protocol that eliminates confounding variation (\S\ref{sec:methods1}), drawing on best practices from clinical trial design \citep{schulz2010consort} and trustworthy online experimentation \citep{kohavi2020trustworthy,kohavi2009experiments}; a cross-generation transferability analysis for assessing whether findings from one model generation transfer to the next (\S\ref{sec:methods2}); and a pre-registered multi-arm RCT adapted from clinical trial methodology, including Bonferroni correction for six hypotheses, O'Brien-Fleming interim boundaries \citep{obrien1979}, and sequential testing principles \citep{wald1945}.

\subsection{The Model Selection Problem for Front-Door Routing}
\label{sec:model-selection}

The model routing problem has emerged as a central challenge in production LLM deployment, and we identify four dominant architectural paradigms. \emph{Learned routers} train on preference data to predict which model will produce a better response for a given prompt. \citet{jiang2023routellm} achieve $2\times$ cost reduction on MT-Bench while maintaining 95\% of GPT-4 quality with a BERT-scale classifier \citep{devlin2019bert}; \citet{ong2025routellm} extend this to an open-source framework; and \citet{hu2024routerbench} provide a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating such multi-LLM routing systems. \emph{Benchmark-guided routing} \citep{shnitzer2023large} selects the model with the highest predicted performance on a prompt's task type using benchmark scores as a prior, building on holistic evaluation frameworks \citep{liang2023helm,srivastava2023beyond}. \emph{Cascade architectures} \citep{chen2023frugalgpt,madaan2023automix,yue2024cascades,lu2023routing} invoke cheaper models first and escalate to expensive models only when confidence is low, differing from our single-pass front-door approach. \emph{Meta-modeling approaches} \citep{sakota2024flyswat,zhang2024onthefly} predict cost-effectiveness of each candidate for a given input; these are conceptually closest to our work but operate at the model-selection level rather than the task-classification level. Speculative decoding \citep{leviathan2023speculative,chen2023speculative}, Branch-Train-Merge \citep{li2022branchtrain}, and Mixture-of-Agents \citep{wang2024moa} address inference efficiency through parallel execution rather than routing.

Our work addresses a specific variant that we term \emph{front-door classification}: a single SLM classifies each incoming prompt into a discrete task taxonomy, and the taxonomy label deterministically selects the downstream model and compression policy. This differs from learned routing in three ways. First, the classification label is human-readable, auditable, and overridable---learned routers produce opaque preference scores. Second, the taxonomy label can trigger multiple downstream decisions (model tier, compression ratio, output budget, quality gate) through a lookup table, whereas learned routers optimize for a single downstream metric. Third, the taxonomy is version-controlled and can be updated without retraining. The tradeoff is that front-door classification requires a well-defined taxonomy with clear boundaries; our 6-family taxonomy (\texttt{code/simple}, \texttt{code/complex}, \texttt{CoT/simple}, \texttt{CoT/complex}, \texttt{hybrid/agentic}, \texttt{hybrid/generative}) is derived from the Article~1 dichotomy extended with complexity and modality dimensions.

\textbf{SLMs as classifiers.}\quad The use of SLMs in the 1--4B parameter range as task classifiers is motivated by several factors: classification requires understanding prompt \emph{intent} rather than generating a complete response, potentially requiring less parametric capacity \citep{kaplan2020scaling}; the output budget is minimal (a single JSON label), eliminating the output token dynamics that dominate inference cost for generative tasks \citep{willard2023outlines,zheng2024outlines}; and SLMs can be self-hosted on commodity GPUs, avoiding API dependencies and data-residency concerns. However, SLMs face known limitations. The Phi family \citep{abdin2024phi3,abdin2024phi4} achieves strong performance on structured tasks but may struggle with ambiguous prompts. The Qwen family \citep{yang2024qwen2,yang2024qwen25} exhibits strong multilingual capabilities but has received less evaluation on classification-specific tasks. DeepSeek's MoE architecture \citep{liu2024deepseek,dai2024deepseek} activates only 37B of its 671B parameters per token, but the internal routing decision may interact with the classification task unpredictably \citep{shazeer2017moe,fedus2022switch,lepikhin2021gshard}. Recent surveys of small language models \citep{lu2024slmsurvey,wang2024slmsurvey} provide comprehensive coverage but do not specifically address classification-for-routing. The Orca \citep{mukherjee2023orca,mitra2023orca2} and WizardLM \citep{xu2023wizardlm} lines of work demonstrate that smaller models can acquire sophisticated reasoning through progressive learning from stronger models, and parameter-efficient adaptation methods such as prefix tuning \citep{li2021prefix} and prompt tuning \citep{lester2021prompt} offer low-cost specialization paths. Production deployment of SLM classifiers requires 4-bit quantization to fit within GPU memory budgets on commodity hardware (T4, A10); recent work on QLoRA \citep{dettmers2023qlora}, GPTQ \citep{frantar2023gptq}, AWQ \citep{lin2024awq}, and SmoothQuant \citep{xiao2023smoothquant} has demonstrated that 4-bit quantization preserves most quality for generative tasks, but the impact on classification accuracy specifically is less well-characterized.

\subsection{Research Questions and Hypotheses}
\label{sec:hypotheses}

The overarching research question is: \emph{Which small language model backend maximizes front-door classification accuracy while satisfying latency and cost constraints for production routing?} We decompose this into hypotheses organized by study.

\textbf{Study~1 hypotheses (validated).}

\begin{hypothesis}[H1: Quality Advantage]
\label{hyp:quality}
\emph{Under the locked \texttt{jsonsafe\_cls\_tokens\_128} contract, Phi-3.5-mini achieves significantly higher exact-label accuracy than Qwen2.5-1.5B on the 60-case corpus.}
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{hypothesis}[H2: Latency Advantage]
\label{hyp:latency-s1}
\emph{Qwen2.5-1.5B achieves significantly lower per-case inference latency than Phi-3.5-mini under identical hardware conditions.}
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{hypothesis}[H3: Task-Family Interaction]
\label{hyp:family}
\emph{The quality advantage is not uniform across task families: different SLMs dominate different families, producing a ``split policy'' rather than a single winner.}
\end{hypothesis}

\textbf{Study~2 hypotheses (validated).}

\begin{hypothesis}[H4: Generational Jump]
\label{hyp:gen-jump}
\emph{Qwen-2.5-3B-Instruct achieves higher classification accuracy than both Qwen2.5-1.5B and Phi-3.5-mini from Study~1, resolving the accuracy deficit while preserving sub-2-second latency.}
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{hypothesis}[H5: Family Coverage]
\label{hyp:coverage}
\emph{The generational jump improves accuracy on families where Qwen2.5-1.5B scored 0.0 (CoT/simple, CoT/complex, code/simple), narrowing the family-level performance gap.}
\end{hypothesis}

\textbf{Study~3 hypotheses (pre-registered, pending execution).}

\begin{hypothesis}[H6: Routing Accuracy]
\label{hyp:routing}
\emph{Routing accuracy differs across arms, with expected ordering $D > C \geq B > A$. Success threshold: $\geq 85\%$ for the winning arm.}
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{hypothesis}[H7: Total Cost]
\label{hyp:cost}
\emph{Average USD per request (front-door $+$ downstream) differs, with $B$ or $C < D < A$. Threshold: $\geq 25\%$ reduction vs.\ Arm~A.}
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{hypothesis}[H8: Classification F1]
\label{hyp:f1}
\emph{F1 macro and hybrid/agentic-specific F1 differ, with $D > C \geq B$. Threshold: F1 macro $\geq 0.82$.}
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{hypothesis}[H9: Latency SLA]
\label{hyp:latency-rct}
\emph{P95 front-door latency differs, with $C \leq B < D$ (network penalty). Hard gate: $\leq 2{,}000$\,ms for all arms.}
\end{hypothesis}

\begin{hypothesis}[H10: Compression Quality]
\label{hyp:compression}
\emph{Average compression ratio at semantic similarity $\geq 0.88$ differs by model backend. Threshold: $\geq 30\%$ token reduction.}
\end{hypothesis}

\section{Methods: Study~1 (Pilot Benchmark)}
\label{sec:methods1}

The pilot study uses a locked corpus and configuration to establish baseline quality-latency profiles for two candidate SLMs under identical conditions. This section describes the experimental contract---the complete specification that must be held invariant for results to be valid---the models under evaluation, the classification system prompt, and the statistical analysis plan.

\textbf{Formal problem statement.}\quad We define the front-door classification task as follows. Let $\mathcal{P}$ denote the space of natural-language prompts and let the label set be
\[
  \mathcal{L} = \{\texttt{cd/smp},\;\texttt{cd/cplx},\;\texttt{CoT/smp},\;\texttt{CoT/cplx},\;\texttt{hyb/agnt},\;\texttt{hyb/gen}\}.
\]
A front-door classifier is a function
\begin{equation}
  f: \mathcal{P} \longrightarrow \mathcal{L} \times [0,1],
  \label{eq:classifier}
\end{equation}
mapping each prompt $p$ to a predicted label $\hat{\ell} = f(p)_{\text{label}} \in \mathcal{L}$ and a self-reported confidence $c = f(p)_{\text{conf}} \in [0,1]$. The downstream routing table $R: \mathcal{L} \to \mathcal{M} \times \Theta$ maps each label to a (model, compression-policy) pair, making the classification decision the single point of control for the entire inference pipeline.

\textbf{Experimental contract.}\quad The contract identifier is \texttt{jsonsafe\_cls\_tokens\_128}. The corpus is \texttt{progressive\_test\_cases\_v2\_60.jsonl} (60 prompts, 6 families $\times$ 10 cases each; SHA-256: \texttt{3be755e8\ldots}). Decoding: \texttt{max\_new\_tokens}=128 (classifier), 24 (scorer), $T = 0.0$ (greedy). Quantization is 4-bit NF4 via bitsandbytes for both models. Fairness invariants require the same host class, commit hash, parser path, and no mid-pair drift. The two models represent opposite ends of the SLM design space: Phi-3.5-mini-instruct (3.8B, dense, Microsoft) optimizes for instruction-following quality; Qwen2.5-1.5B-Instruct (1.5B, dense, Alibaba) optimizes for minimal footprint.

\textbf{Classification system prompt.}\quad Both models receive an identical, frozen classification system prompt:

\begin{quote}
\small
\begin{verbatim}
You are a task classifier for an AI routing system.
Classify the prompt into exactly one of these categories:
- code/simple (single function, snippet, trivial script)
- code/complex (multi-file, architecture-level, tests required)
- CoT/simple (single-step explanation or reasoning)
- CoT/complex (multi-step reasoning, trade-off analysis)
- hybrid/agentic (autonomous execution, self-healing,
  multi-artifact, no-confirmation)
- hybrid/generative (mixed creative + structured output)

Key rule: autonomous mode + no confirmation + multi-artifact = hybrid/agentic.

Respond with JSON only: {"label": "<label>", "confidence": <0.0-1.0>}
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}

This prompt was frozen as of \texttt{benchmark\_openweights.py} line 347 and used identically across all three studies. The decision to use a zero-shot prompt without chain-of-thought reasoning \citep{wei2022chain,kojima2022zeroshotreasoners} was deliberate: chain-of-thought prompting for classification would increase output token count and latency, potentially negating the front-door's cost advantage. Few-shot prompting \citep{brown2020language,min2022incontext} was similarly excluded to maintain prompt brevity and cross-model comparability.

\textbf{Inference framework.}\quad Study~1 used HuggingFace \texttt{transformers} with \texttt{model.generate()} for both models, not a production serving engine. This unoptimized inference path is a known limitation that likely inflates Phi-3.5-mini's 12-second latency measurements; a production deployment using vLLM \citep{kwon2023vllm} or TensorRT-LLM would substantially reduce latency for both models.

\textbf{Execution environment.}\quad RunPod Secure Cloud, NVIDIA A40 (48 GB GDDR6, dedicated). Pod ID \texttt{4vhwewr5sy3zfc}, hostname \texttt{d0f8aedc2cea}, branch \texttt{slm-front-door-experiement} at commit \texttt{07771b20} (2026-03-12).

\textbf{Statistical analysis plan.}\quad The primary endpoint is exact label correctness per case (paired, binary), tested with exact McNemar two-sided \citep{mcnemar1947}. This is the strictest possible metric---a partially correct label (e.g., correct task type but wrong complexity level) receives zero credit. Secondary endpoints are parse success (paired binary, exact McNemar) and per-case latency (paired continuous, Wilcoxon signed-rank \citep{wilcoxon1945}). Confidence intervals use the bootstrap percentile method with 10,000 resamples \citep{efron1979bootstrap}. Family-level analyses are exploratory (10 cases per family) and are reported without multiple-comparison correction.

\section{Results: Study~1}
\label{sec:results1}

Given a labeled evaluation set $\mathcal{D} = \{(p_i, \ell_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}$, we measure exact-match accuracy as
\begin{equation}
  \text{Acc}(f, \mathcal{D}) \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}|} \sum_{(p,\ell)\in\mathcal{D}} \ind\bigl[f(p)_{\text{label}} = \ell\bigr],
  \label{eq:accuracy}
\end{equation}
where $\ind[\cdot]$ is the indicator function. This is the strictest possible metric: partial matches (e.g., correct task type but wrong complexity) receive zero credit.

Table~\ref{tab:pilot-overall} presents the overall 60-case pairwise benchmark results. $N = 60$ paired cases yielded 33 discordant correctness pairs (cases where exactly one model was correct). Of these, 26 favored Phi-3.5-mini and 7 favored Qwen2.5-1.5B, producing an accuracy difference $\Delta = +0.3167$ (Phi $-$ Qwen) with 95\% bootstrap CI $[0.15, 0.4833]$ (10,000 resamples, percentile method). Exact McNemar two-sided $p = 0.0013187$. Cohen's $h = +0.677$ (medium-large effect). Both models achieved perfect JSON parse rates (1.0000), confirming that the \texttt{jsonsafe\_cls\_tokens\_128} contract successfully constrains output format. The latency distribution is dramatically separated: Hodges-Lehmann estimate of location shift \citep{hodges1963} is 11,061\,ms in favor of Qwen2.5-1.5B (Wilcoxon signed-rank $p = 1.63 \times 10^{-11}$). The $7.9\times$ latency ratio (Phi/Qwen) reflects the $2.5\times$ parameter count difference (3.8B vs.\ 1.5B) amplified by 4-bit quantization memory access patterns on A40. As noted in \S\ref{sec:methods1}, the 12-second Phi latency likely reflects the unoptimized HuggingFace \texttt{model.generate()} inference path rather than the model's inherent throughput; a vLLM or TensorRT-LLM deployment would substantially reduce this figure.

\begin{finding}[Quality-Latency Tradeoff]
\label{find:tradeoff}
\emph{Phi-3.5-mini provides $2.6\times$ higher classification accuracy than Qwen2.5-1.5B ($p = 0.0013$), at $7.9\times$ higher latency ($p = 1.63 \times 10^{-11}$). Neither model meets the 85\% production deployment threshold for standalone routing.}
\end{finding}

\textbf{Family-level behavior.}\quad The family-level results in Table~\ref{tab:pilot-family} reveal a striking pattern of complementary specialization, visualized in Figure~\ref{fig:family-bar}. Phi-3.5-mini achieves perfect or near-perfect accuracy on \texttt{code/complex} (1.0), \texttt{CoT/complex} (1.0), and \texttt{CoT/simple} (0.8), but fails on \texttt{hybrid/agentic} (0.3). Conversely, Qwen2.5-1.5B achieves perfect accuracy on \texttt{hybrid/agentic} (1.0) but fails on all CoT families (0.0) and most code families. \section{Methods: Study~2 (Generational Bridge Experiment)}
\label{sec:methods2}

Study~1 established that the accuracy-latency tradeoff space contains two insufficient extremes: Phi-3.5-mini (high accuracy, high latency) and Qwen2.5-1.5B (low accuracy, low latency). A natural question is whether a single \emph{generation jump}---doubling the parameter count within the same model family---can shift a candidate closer to the production deployment threshold (accuracy $\geq 0.85$, latency $\leq 2{,}000$\,ms). Study~2 tests this hypothesis with Qwen-2.5-3B-Instruct.

\textbf{Experimental contract.}\quad The Study~2 contract mirrors Study~1 as closely as possible, with explicitly noted deviations. The model is Qwen-2.5-3B-Instruct \citep{yang2024qwen25} (3B parameters, 4-bit NF4 via bitsandbytes), served by vLLM 0.17.1 \citep{kwon2023vllm} with bitsandbytes 4-bit quantization backend. The system prompt is identical to Study~1 (\S\ref{sec:methods1}). Decoding parameters are \texttt{max\_new\_tokens}=128, temperature $T = 0.0$. The corpus is a reconstructed \texttt{progressive\_test\_cases\_v2\_60.jsonl} (SHA-256: \texttt{dac3aac5\ldots}). This SHA differs from the Study~1 corpus (\texttt{3be755e8\ldots}) due to corpus reconstruction from source materials after a storage migration; manual inspection confirmed that all 60 prompts and gold labels match the Study~1 corpus content, but we cannot cryptographically guarantee byte-level identity. We flag this as a known limitation and report Study~2 results alongside---but not pooled with---Study~1 results. Cross-study accuracy comparisons should therefore be treated as approximate rather than exact.

\textbf{Execution environment.}\quad Microsoft Azure, NVIDIA T4 (16 GB GDDR6), shared GPU allocation (not dedicated). MLflow Run ID: \texttt{2e07a00548434aae816f4599a66e03af}. Date: 2026-03-14. The shared GPU allocation means other workloads may have contended for memory bandwidth during execution; latency measurements from Study~2 are therefore \textbf{not directly comparable} to Study~1 latency measurements from the dedicated RunPod A40. We report Study~2 latency as a within-run descriptive statistic only. Accuracy comparisons across studies are valid; latency comparisons are not, due to hardware, framework, and allocation differences.

\textbf{Inference framework confound.}\quad Study~1 used HuggingFace \texttt{transformers} with \texttt{model.generate()}, while Study~2 uses vLLM 0.17.1. Beyond the expected latency difference (vLLM implements PagedAttention and continuous batching), the two frameworks can theoretically produce different classification outputs for the same model and prompt due to differences in attention computation precision, KV-cache management, and sampling implementation. At temperature $T = 0.0$ with \texttt{max\_new\_tokens}=128, the divergence risk is low for short JSON outputs, but it remains a potential confound. We note this limitation but cannot quantify it without a runtime parity check (running both frameworks on the same model and corpus), which was not performed for Study~2.

\textbf{Statistical analysis plan.}\quad Because Study~2 evaluates a single model (no paired comparison), we report descriptive accuracy with binomial exact 95\% CIs and compare to Study~1 baselines using unpaired methods. Cross-study comparisons use independent-samples tests (Fisher's exact test for proportions \citep{fisher1922}) rather than paired tests, reflecting the different execution environments and corpus SHA.

\section{Study~3: Four-Arm RCT Design}
\label{sec:rct-design}

Studies~1 and~2 established offline quality-latency profiles. Study~3 translates these findings into a production-grade randomized controlled trial that evaluates front-door routing under real traffic conditions with endpoint isolation, cost attribution, and pre-registered decision criteria.

\textbf{Trial arms.}\quad Table~\ref{tab:arms} defines the four treatment arms. Arm~A (no routing) provides the baseline against which routing value is measured. Note that Arm~A routing accuracy is undefined (no router is invoked), so comparisons involving Arm~A test cost and quality \emph{impact} of routing, while treatment-vs-treatment comparisons (B vs.\ C, B vs.\ D, C vs.\ D) test relative routing accuracy. Arm~B tests Phi-4-mini-instruct \citep{abdin2024phi4}, the next-generation Phi model and expected successor to the Study~1 Phi-3.5-mini. Arm~C tests Qwen-2.5-3B, validated in Study~2 as the most promising offline candidate. Arm~D tests whether a full-scale MoE model (671B, 37B active) provides classification quality improvements that justify its API dependency and cost---with particular attention to the output instability documented in Articles~4--5 for the DeepSeek architecture. The inclusion of a commercial API arm alongside self-hosted arms also tests whether network latency, rate limiting, and vendor uptime affect real-world routing performance in ways that offline benchmarks cannot capture.

\textbf{Randomization and integrity.}\quad Assignment uses SHA-256(\texttt{session\_id}) $\bmod 4$, deterministic with no mid-session switches, following \citet{kohavi2020trustworthy}. Stratification is by prompt complexity quartile, targeting equal arm representation within $\pm 5\%$. The design is single-blind: callers do not know arm assignment; the front-door endpoint URL is identical across Arms~B, C, D. Classification, scoring, and compression prompts are identical across Arms~B, C, D.

\textbf{Sample size and power.}\quad The target is $N = 250$ per arm ($N_{\text{total}} = 1{,}000$) with a minimum detectable effect of 10 percentage points (informed by the $+31.7$\,pp Study~1 delta). Significance is set at $\alpha = 0.05$, Bonferroni-corrected to $\alpha/6 = 0.0083$ for six pairwise comparisons \citep{bonferroni1936}. We acknowledge that $N = 250$ per arm achieves approximately 61\% power at the corrected $\alpha = 0.0083$ for a 10\,pp effect at baseline accuracy $\sim$0.767, below the conventional 80\% threshold. For smaller differences (5\,pp), power drops to approximately 10\%, meaning the study is substantially underpowered to detect fine-grained accuracy differences between treatment arms. We pre-commit to the Holm-Bonferroni step-down procedure as the primary correction method, which provides higher power while still controlling the family-wise error rate. If the Holm-Bonferroni-corrected results remain inconclusive, we will additionally report Dunnett's test, which compares each of three treatment arms against the control (3 comparisons instead of 6), yielding a less conservative threshold of $\alpha/3 = 0.0167$ and correspondingly higher power. If both approaches yield null results, we will increase to $N = 400$ per arm. Interim analysis uses O'Brien-Fleming spending function boundaries at $N = 125$ per arm \citep{obrien1979}, following \citet{wald1945} and \citet{johari2017peeking}. Arms may be dropped at interim if clearly futile.

\textbf{Multiplicity hierarchy.}\quad The Bonferroni correction $\alpha/6 = 0.0083$ applies \emph{within} each hypothesis to the six pairwise arm comparisons (A vs.\ B, A vs.\ C, A vs.\ D, B vs.\ C, B vs.\ D, C vs.\ D). Between hypotheses (H6--H10), which test different endpoints (routing accuracy, cost, F1, latency, compression quality), no additional correction is applied. This is standard practice for multi-arm clinical trials where hypotheses address distinct endpoints \citep{schulz2010consort}: the multiplicity concern arises from testing the same endpoint across multiple arm pairs, not from testing different endpoints.

\textbf{Statistical analysis plan.}\quad Primary tests are: routing accuracy, defect rate, and F1 via $\chi^2$ test for proportions \citep{cochran1954} with Bonferroni correction; cost and compression ratio via Welch's $t$-test \citep{welch1947} with Bonferroni correction; P95 latency via bootstrap CI comparison \citep{efron1979bootstrap}. Subgroup analysis by task-label is exploratory (not Bonferroni-corrected, flagged if $p < 0.01$). Pareto frontier analysis is computed over (total\_cost\_per\_request, routing\_accuracy), extending the Article~6 methodology from (cost, fidelity) to (cost, routing\_accuracy).

\textbf{Cost-savings formalization.}\quad We formalize the break-even condition for routing following the Article~6 framework \citep{johnson2026rct}. For a stream of requests $t = 1, \ldots, T$, the net routing savings are
\begin{equation}
  S \;=\; \sum_{t=1}^{T} \bigl[C_{\text{auto}}(t) - C_{\text{routed}}(t)\bigr],
  \label{eq:savings}
\end{equation}
where $C_{\text{auto}}(t)$ is the cost of sending request $t$ to the default (most-expensive) model and $C_{\text{routed}}(t) = c_{\text{fd}} + C_{R(\hat{\ell}_t)}(t)$ includes the front-door classifier cost $c_{\text{fd}}$ plus the cost of the model selected by the routing table $R$. On correctly classified requests, $C_{\text{routed}} \ll C_{\text{auto}}$ for simple tasks; on misclassified requests, the downstream model may produce low-quality output requiring a retry at full cost. The break-even accuracy $a^*$ satisfies $S(a^*) = 0$; above this threshold, routing is cost-positive.

\textbf{Pre-registered decision matrix.}\quad Table~\ref{tab:decision} maps RCT outcomes to deployment actions. The decision matrix is grounded in Pareto dominance over the two primary objectives:

\begin{proposition}[Pareto Dominance]
\label{prop:pareto}
\emph{Arm $i$ dominates arm $j$ if and only if}
\[
  \mathrm{cost}_i \leq \mathrm{cost}_j \quad \text{and} \quad \mathrm{acc}_i \geq \mathrm{acc}_j,
\]
\emph{with at least one strict inequality. Arms on the Pareto frontier are those not dominated by any other arm.}
\end{proposition}

The Pareto analysis is a decision-support tool operating on point estimates; statistical significance of pairwise differences is established separately via the hypothesis tests (H6--H10).

When two frontier arms are statistically indistinguishable on primary metrics, the tie-breaking rule prefers the arm with fewer external dependencies---self-hosted (C) $>$ serverless (B) $>$ API (D)---reflecting operational preferences for data residency and vendor independence.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The results of Studies~1 and~2, viewed together, illuminate several fundamental tensions in the design of production front-door routing systems. This section integrates the empirical findings with the broader literature to develop a coherent account of where the field stands and where it must go.

\textbf{The split policy evolves across generations.}\quad The most striking finding across Studies~1 and~2 is the \emph{evolution} of the split policy. In Study~1, the split was dramatic: Phi dominated code/CoT families while Qwen dominated hybrid-agentic (Finding~\ref{find:split}). In Study~2, Qwen-2.5-3B substantially narrows this gap, achieving $\geq 0.70$ on all families including those where its predecessor scored 0.0. This evolution has a concrete implication for the Article~1 dichotomy \citep{johnson2026compress}: Article~1 showed that the optimal \emph{cost strategy} (compress vs.\ route) depends on task type; the pilot benchmark showed that the optimal \emph{router model} also depends on task type; but Study~2 suggests that this recursive dependency may be a \emph{transient} artifact of model immaturity. As SLM classifiers improve, the split policy may converge toward a single-model solution. The convergence hypothesis is particularly important for operational simplicity, since a split policy requires a \emph{meta-router} that first determines which family a prompt belongs to before selecting the appropriate classifier, creating an infinite regress problem. If generational improvement eliminates the need for split routing, the production system simplifies from a two-tier to a single-tier classification architecture, reducing latency, complexity, and maintenance burden---consistent with \citeauthor{sculley2015debt}'s (\citeyear{sculley2015debt}) observation that hidden technical debt in ML systems accumulates fastest at integration boundaries. The evolution also raises a deeper question about whether the split policy reflects genuine architectural specialization or merely uneven training data coverage; resolving this would require controlled experiments with models trained on identical data but with different architectures, an investigation beyond the scope of this paper but methodologically feasible within the Orca-style training paradigm \citep{mukherjee2023orca,mitra2023orca2}.

\textbf{Scaling laws for classification.}\quad The accuracy trajectory across the three evaluated models---Qwen2.5-1.5B (0.200), Phi-3.5-mini (0.517), Qwen-2.5-3B (0.767)---is suggestive of a scaling relationship between parameter count and classification accuracy, though the trajectory confounds parameter count with model family, training data, and architecture improvements. A simple log-linear model fit to the three data points gives
\begin{equation}
  \text{Acc}(N) \approx \hat{\alpha} \cdot \ln\bigl(N/N_0\bigr), \qquad \hat{\alpha} = 0.81, \quad \hat{N}_0 = 0.87\text{B},
  \label{eq:scaling}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the parameter count in billions. The fit ($R^2 = 0.97$) has zero predictive value---three points can fit almost any monotonic function with $R^2 > 0.95$. It is included solely to frame quantitative expectations for the RCT, not to assert a scaling law for classification accuracy.

\citet{kaplan2020scaling} established power-law scaling for generative loss; whether classification accuracy follows similar scaling is an open question. \citet{wei2022emergent} documented emergent abilities that appear abruptly above a critical scale, which could explain the dramatic jump from 0.200 to 0.767 within the Qwen family---the 1.5B model may lack a critical capacity for multi-dimensional task taxonomy reasoning that emerges between 1.5B and 3B parameters. However, \citet{schaeffer2024mirage} have argued that apparent emergent abilities may be artifacts of evaluation metric choices, suggesting caution. The Chinchilla scaling laws \citep{hoffmann2022chinchilla} suggest the accuracy improvement from 1.5B to 3B reflects the combined effect of more parameters and more training data. For the practical question of which model to deploy, the combined effect is what matters: the 3B model demonstrably classifies better while maintaining acceptable latency. Our results suggest that a 3B model may be approaching a ``classification ceiling'' for our 6-family taxonomy, given that the remaining errors are concentrated in genuinely ambiguous boundary cases---a diminishing-returns hypothesis that, if confirmed, would validate the economic premise of the front-door architecture by establishing that the optimal classifier size is substantially smaller than the optimal size for downstream generative tasks.

\textbf{The accuracy-latency Pareto frontier.}\quad Figure~\ref{fig:scatter} visualizes the three evaluated models in accuracy-latency space. Qwen-2.5-3B approaches but does not enter the viable region (accuracy $\geq 0.85$, latency $\leq 2{,}000$\,ms). Three paths could push a candidate into the viable region: (1) prompt engineering via chain-of-thought \citep{wei2022chain,wang2023selfconsistency} or tree-of-thought \citep{yao2024treeofthoughts}, at the cost of increased output tokens and latency; (2) fine-tuning via LoRA \citep{hu2022lora} on a classification-specific dataset; or (3) next-generation models. The RCT implicitly tests path~(3) by including Phi-4-mini. A fourth path---inference infrastructure optimization via PagedAttention \citep{kwon2023vllm}, FlashAttention \citep{dao2022flashattention,dao2023flashattention2}, or disaggregated serving \citep{zhong2024distserve}---could reduce latency without changing accuracy, shifting all points leftward on the plot and potentially enabling multiple classification passes (a form of self-consistency \citep{wang2023selfconsistency}) to boost accuracy toward 0.85.

\textbf{Connection to provider-dependent dynamics.}\quad Articles~4--5 \citep{johnson2026greenai,johnson2026benchmark} documented that DeepSeek-Chat exhibits compression sensitivity CS $= -54.1$ ($38\times$ output explosion). The proposed RCT includes DeepSeek-V3 (Arm~D) with the same architectural lineage. The classification task differs from generative tasks in that the output budget is tightly constrained (\texttt{max\_new\_tokens}=128), which may mitigate explosion risk, but Article~5's model-family-dependence finding suggests caution. The MoE architecture \citep{shazeer2017moe,fedus2022switch,dai2024deepseek} raises a particularly interesting question: for classification, only the final output tokens matter, meaning the MoE's internal routing decisions on intermediate tokens are optimized for generative objectives rather than classification objectives. Whether this misalignment helps or hinders classification accuracy is an empirical question that the RCT will partially address.

\textbf{The tokenizer-awareness tension.}\quad Article~7 \citep{johnson2026promptlang} established that prompt design should account for the target model's tokenizer. The Study~3 design creates a tension with this principle: the classification system prompt is frozen across all three SLM backends, each with a different tokenizer (Phi uses a Llama-derived tokenizer, Qwen uses a custom BPE tokenizer \citep{sennrich2016subword}, DeepSeek uses SentencePiece \citep{kudo2018sentencepiece}). We chose the frozen-prompt design to isolate model capability from prompt engineering and to reflect the practical deployment scenario where a single prompt is maintained for all backends. If the RCT reveals large quality differences, a follow-up study should assess how much of the gap is attributable to tokenizer mismatch, given that even small prompt variations can produce large accuracy differences \citep{zhou2023prompt,khattab2024dspy}.

\textbf{Implications for production architecture.}\quad The sub-2-second latency achieved by both Qwen models demonstrates that SLM-based front-door classification is compatible with production latency requirements, adding minimal overhead relative to downstream generative inference (typically 5--30 seconds). The breadth advantage of Qwen-2.5-3B (Finding~\ref{find:breadth}) suggests that for production routing, a single generalist classifier is preferable to a committee of specialists, even if individual specialists achieve higher peak accuracy. The confidence overconfidence pattern argues against using model-reported confidence as a fallback trigger; instead, production systems should rely on external quality signals such as output structural validity or downstream task completion rate \citep{klaise2021alibi}. These architectural considerations intersect with the broader MLOps literature on model management and technical debt \citep{sculley2015debt,kreuzberger2023mlops}. The ideal front-door classifier would be robust to downstream model changes, requiring updates only when the task taxonomy itself evolves; our frozen-prompt, frozen-contract design approximates this ideal, but the extent to which it generalizes to new model families remains open.

\section{Threats to Validity}
\label{sec:threats}

\textbf{Internal validity.}\quad Study~1 used a dedicated RunPod A40; Study~2 used a shared Azure T4. Latency measurements are therefore not cross-study comparable, and accuracy differences could theoretically be influenced by hardware-dependent quantization behavior. We mitigate this by not pooling cross-study data, reporting accuracy as the primary metric, and planning the RCT on uniform hardware. The Study~2 corpus SHA (\texttt{dac3aac5}) differs from Study~1 (\texttt{3be755e8}); manual inspection confirmed content equivalence, but this remains a potential confound. Studies~1 and~2 were executed two days apart (March 12 and 14, 2026), and library versions differ (vLLM 0.17.1 in Study~2 vs.\ direct HuggingFace inference in Study~1).

\textbf{External validity.}\quad The 60-case corpus represents a structured sample from six pre-defined task families. Production traffic may have different family distributions, prompt lengths, and ambiguity characteristics; the RCT ($N = 250$ per arm) will use production traffic. The 6-family taxonomy is specific to the \plexor{} routing system; other architectures using different taxonomies could produce different rankings. All results use 4-bit NF4 quantization; full-precision results may differ.

\textbf{Construct validity.}\quad Exact-match label accuracy is the strictest possible metric; partial credit might favor different models, but production routing applies discrete policies per label. The model's self-reported confidence score is not calibrated and should not be interpreted as a probability. All results depend on the specific frozen system prompt, output budget, and quantization configuration.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper reports three complementary studies addressing the model-selection problem for production front-door routing. Study~1 established, under a locked hardware and decoding contract, that Phi-3.5-mini provides significantly higher classification quality than Qwen2.5-1.5B ($\Delta = +0.317$, $p = 0.0013$; Cohen's $h = +0.677$) at $7.9\times$ higher latency, producing a split policy where the optimal router depends on the task family. Neither pilot-generation model meets the 0.85 accuracy threshold for standalone deployment. Study~2 demonstrated that a single generation jump within the Qwen family (1.5B $\to$ 3B) achieves 0.767 accuracy ($+0.567$ over the 1.5B predecessor, $+0.250$ over Phi-3.5-mini), with perfect parse rates and sub-2-second latency. The breadth-over-depth finding---that the minimum of a model's family-level accuracies matters more than the maximum for production routing---inverts the typical evaluation paradigm where peak benchmark performance dominates attention. Study~3 provides a pre-registered four-arm RCT design with Bonferroni-corrected pairwise tests, O'Brien-Fleming interim analysis, and a decision matrix ensuring deployment recommendations follow mechanically from data.

Together, these studies demonstrate an end-to-end methodology for evidence-based model selection: locked offline benchmarking, generational validation, and pre-registered production experimentation. The broader implication for the \taac{} research program is that the Article~1 dichotomy (compress code, route CoT) extends recursively---the model that implements the routing decision is itself subject to task-dependent quality-latency tradeoffs. As SLM classifiers improve, this recursive dependency may simplify toward a single-model solution. Whether the RCT outcome favors a single generalist, a specialist committee, or a hybrid architecture, the pre-registered decision matrix ensures that the recommendation will be driven by evidence rather than intuition---a standard that production ML experimentation would benefit from adopting more broadly \citep{kohavi2020trustworthy,nosek2018preregistration}.

Beyond the specific empirical results, this work contributes to the emerging paradigm of \emph{SLM-as-classifier} for production inference routing. The finding that a 3B-parameter model can achieve 76.7\% exact-match accuracy on a 6-class taxonomy with sub-2-second latency suggests that the front-door architecture is approaching viability with current-generation models, though the 0.767 accuracy on a 60-case reconstructed corpus falls short of the 0.85 production threshold and has not yet been validated under production traffic conditions. This trajectory has significant implications for the design of cost-optimized LLM inference systems: rather than investing in increasingly sophisticated learned routers that require preference data and periodic retraining, practitioners may achieve comparable or superior routing quality through the simpler approach of deploying the latest SLM with a frozen classification prompt. The pre-registered RCT (Study~3) will provide the first production-traffic validation of this hypothesis. If confirmed, the SLM-as-classifier paradigm would represent a substantial simplification of the inference routing stack---reducing it from a trained ML pipeline to a single model invocation with a versioned prompt, dramatically lowering the operational complexity and technical debt burden that \citet{sculley2015debt} identified as the dominant cost in production ML systems.
